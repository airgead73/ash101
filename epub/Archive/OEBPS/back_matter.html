Glossary

academic honesty A code of conduct that ensures student work is original and ethical; it involves transparently acknowledging the ideas and works of others.

academic integrity A moral code in academia that emphasizes sound judgment anda personal sense of ethical responsibility.

active reading The process of critically engaging with a text.

annotating Writing a note, either in the margin of a text or on a separate document, that explains or comments on what you have read.

applicant tracking system (ATS) Software used by hiring managers to review résumés and narrow down the number of applicants for a given position.

brain-based learning The application ofscientific knowledge about brain functioning to teaching and learning methods.

career road map A deliberate plan for your career path and career development.

casual register The language of friendsand acquaintances that often uses private, shared vocabulary and slang and typically does not require the sharing of background information.

classical conditioning The repeated pairing of two stimuli so that they become linked; eventually, exposure to only one of the stimuli can elicit the same response as when both were present.

Cornell method A note-taking method in which you take a standard piece of notebook paper and divide it into three main sections: a summary area, a questions column, and a notes column.

daily top three A time- and task-management strategy in which you determine the three things that are the most important to finish on a given day and designate them as the tasks that you will complete.

diversity In the context of the college experience, the ways in which those with whom you interact differ by race, culture, age, ethnicity, religion, socioeconomic status, sexual orientation, abilities, opinions, political views, and other factors.

eat the frog A time- and task-management strategy based on the concept that if a person takes care of the biggest or most unpleasant task first, everything else will be easier after that.

extrinsic motivation A desire to do something that is driven by external factors. This type of motivation takes two forms: the desire to seek reward or the desire to avoid punishment.

fabrication The intentional falsification or invention of any information or citation in an academic exercise.

consultative register A version of the formal register used in less structured conversations.

fixed mindset The belief that one is either smart or dumb and that there is little opportunity to grow or change one’s abilities or talents.

focused attention The brain’s ability to concentrate on one specific task at a time.

formal register Also called “academic English,” this is the language of work and school. It uses extensive vocabulary, correct syntax and spelling, and complete, grammatically correct sentence structure.

frozen register A level of language that does not change, including oaths, biblicalquotations, and famous sayings.

grit Sustained, personal perseverance toward a task or goal, even when challenges arise.

growth mindset The belief that one’s talents and abilities can be improved with effort.

hard skills Abilities or skill sets that a person can learn or be trained to obtain.

inclusivity The practice of including those who may otherwise be excluded.

intimate register The language usedbetween lovers and close family members. Intonation is more important than wording or grammar.

intrinsic motivation A desire to do something that is driven by internal factors, such as values, priorities, and interests.

mapping A note-taking method that uses a graphic depiction to make connections between main ideas.

memory The ability to retain and retrieve information about past events and experiences.

metacognition The practice of thinking about how you think.

multitasking Doing several things at the same time.

negative bias The psychological trait of focusing on the negative aspects of a situation rather than the positive.

outlining A note-taking method that uses a numbering and indenting format to show the connections between main ideas, concepts, and supporting details.

personal development plan (PDP) A road map to achieving your goals that includes the steps you need to take and the resources you’ll need to get there.

plagiarism The use of ideas or words from an outside source without proper attribution.

Pomodoro Technique A time- and task-management strategy that uses a timer to set work intervals that are followed by a short break.

priorities The things, people, activities, or even beliefs that you give more of your time or attention to than other things.

procrastination The act of delaying a task that needs to be completed.

recursive reading The process of rereading and annotating a single text over and over.

reflection A mental process in which one thinks seriously about information or actions in order to better understand them.

register of language The idea that the formality of language is a continuum ranging from very formally structured language to very intimate language.

resilience The ability to quickly overcome challenges.

résumé A document presenting one’s employment qualifications, including education, skills, and accomplishments, for the purpose of securing an interview with an intended employer.

rubric An evaluation and scoring tool used to assess the quality of a student’s work.

self-efficacy One’s belief in their capabilityto succeed.

self-reflection The act of thinking aboutone’s own values, decisions, and behaviors.

self-regulation The ability to manage one’s own emotions, thoughts, and behaviors.

shorthand A method of writing quickly using abbreviations and symbols in place of full words or phrases.

SMART goal A goal that is specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time bound.

soft skills Skills that characterize your relationships with other people or dictate how you approach life and work.

stereotype threat A situation in which a member of a social group feels at risk of conforming to stereotypes about that group.

values The basic beliefs that guide our thinking and actions.

visual note-taking A creative approach to note-taking in which you add pictures to your notes for clarity.
