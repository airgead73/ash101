Introduction to Learning and Assessment

I think animal testing is a terrible idea; they get all nervous and give the wrong answers.

—A Bit of Fry & Laurie

People, too, sometimes give the wrong answers on tests because they get nervous and scared. That's what often happened to Jacob. Tests scared the pants off him. When he sat down to take one, he would quickly forget every sensible thought and well-memorized fact he had stuffed into his brain. His test results were a disaster. They greatly chagrined his mother—especially when, following a series of countywide achievement tests, the principal called her in.

"Jacob should probably stop dreaming about going to university and becoming a professor," said the principal. "There's other stuff he can do."

But Jacob didn't stop dreaming. He just kept trying to educate himself. "They can't teach me more stuff just by giving me more tests," he declared. "That's about as sensible as thinking you can make yourself thinner just by weighing yourself a lot."

1.1 Introduction

Focus Question What is this chapter about?

Jacob was a bright young man (his uncle once described him as having a brain as big as a planet). But he suffered from what psychologists label test anxiety.

The mere thought of any sort of assessment, and the fear that he would not do well, invariably led to a frayed emotional state that interfered with Jacob's ability to perform well on tests. As he explained it, school assessments made him feel like a cat that is falling with its back to the ground and just can't seem to get its feet pointed the right way.

Assessment can be like that for some students—highly threatening and anxiety provoking. This is especially the case when the student's performance has profound implications, not only for the learner but perhaps also for the teacher, the school, the parents, and others. Such situations are labeled high-stakes testing environments (these are discussed more in Chapter 10).

But assessment in many of today's schools is very different from what it was in Jacob's school, which at the time was more focused on performance.

In fact, the best current approaches to classroom assessment are designed less to determine how well or how poorly students perform and more to help them learn.

Such approaches to testing might well have uncovered Jacob's problem and helped him before he abandoned all hope.

This chapter looks at current approaches to educational assessment and its various purposes and forms. It explains how assessment is driven by the main, overriding goals of the educational enterprise, how curricula are designed around these goals, and how assessments are used to gauge the extent to which goals are being met. It looks at the implications of various pieces of legislation that direct and influence educational endeavors. It examines some of the rules and regulations designed to ensure that assessments are as fair, useful, and ethical as possible. Finally, it looks at what we know about approaches that work in education.

1.2 Purposes and Types of Assessment

Focus Questions What are the most important purposes of educational assessment? How are summative, formative, and placement assessments different?

Educational assessment has two broad purposes: The most important, strongly reflected in today's assessment literature, is to improve teaching and learning (e.g., Stiggins & Chappuis, 2017). That purpose is emphasized throughout this text. The second purpose is to measure student achievement in order to provide a grade.

Summative Assessment

Assessment that is mainly used to provide a midterm or year-end grade, or to assess mastery at the end of a unit, is called summative assessment.

Summative assessment has traditionally been the main function of testing in schools, partly because its historical emphasis has been on sorting learners to select those with the highest probability of future success. Selecting students is no longer the principal mission of our schools, as Stiggins and Chappuis (2017) explain.

Schools increasingly need to ensure that learners know how to learn—that they become competent, self-directed learners who are actively involved in their own education.

Yesterday's approaches to education do not work well for schools' new mission or for today's students, argues Prensky (2011, 2012a, 2012b).

Today's learners are very different: They are members of what is sometimes labeled the net generation (Oblinger & Oblinger, 2005), also called Generation Z, iGen, post-millennials, or a variety of other terms. These learners are so profoundly influenced by digital technologies (such as the Internet, video games, and computers) that they think and learn differently than did older generations of students. (We look at some characteristics of today's learners and at how these impact learning and assessment in Chapter 3).

Because today's learners are different, and because what we expect of our schools is also different, contemporary emphases in education are changing. They reflect our beliefs and intentions regarding the main purpose of school. Today's model is more egalitarian: It advocates success for all rather than for a chosen few. Furthermore, educators such as Prensky (2016) strongly argue that education should focus less on improving individuals and more on improving the world. In his words, "Kids now need an education that is far more connected and real than in the past—an education that gives them not only knowledge, but also provides them with empowerment and agency" (Prensky, 2016, p. 1).

Accordingly, today's teacher views assessment mainly as a tool to provide guidance and assistance; to make today's student a highly competent, self-motivated, lifelong learner. The hope is that this learner will also be imbued with the motivation and passion to make the world a better place.

Formative Assessment

Assessment designed to improve the teaching/learning process is called formative assessment. It consists of the huge variety of methods teachers use to assess and assist learner progress and comprehension during a lesson, course, or unit.

Put another way, formative assessment is assessment for learning; summative assessment is assessment of learning.

Characteristics

Formative assessment is that which is embedded in the teaching–learning process. Much of it is informal rather than structured and formal. It has the following features:

  • It consists of ongoing strategies such as questions, quizzes, and observation of student performances and products.
  • It involves providing frequent and timely feedback aimed directly at improving teaching and learning.

Instead of summarizing achievement with a number or grade (summative assessment), formative assessment provides information that a teacher and learner can put to use before formal grading occurs.

Key to Assessment in Schools

As Hattie (2012) puts it, "The primary function of assessment is to support learning by generating feedback that students can act upon in terms of where they are going, how they are going there, and where they might go next" (p. 126).

Hence, the primary purpose of assessment in schools is (or should be) formative.

Placement Assessment

As the name implies, placement assessment is designed to provide information relating to a learner's placement.

It typically occurs prior to an instructional sequence and looks at the learner's readiness for a lesson, unit, course, or entire program.

Placement assessment often includes diagnostic assessment, which is directed specifically at identifying learning strengths or problems and their underlying causes. For example, diagnostic assessment might be used to determine whether a learner would benefit from being placed in a group that receives remedial instruction in reading or mathematics.

Of course, the fact that both learners and educational emphases are changing does not mean that all teachers stress the formative role of assessment over its summative uses. Some critics point out that many of today's schools still reflect the approaches and emphases of the past. Groshell (2017), for example, notes that although there have been enormous advances in both technology and educational goals, relatively little has changed regarding how schools view learning and how they design the learning environment. Still, there is little doubt that the uses and purposes of educational assessment are changing—and even less doubt that they should.

Performance-Based Assessment

Another important category of educational assessment is performance-based assessment.

Performance-based assessments ask learners to demonstrate their skills, knowledge, and understanding by actually performing a task or producing a product that directly illustrates what is being assessed.

Thus, performance-based assessments can be used for both formative and summative purposes.

For example, asking first grader William to read aloud from one of the work sheets that accompanies the Beginning Reader series is a performance-based assessment. Judging William's performance according to a rubric, a type of scoring guide described in Chapter 7 and shown in Table 1.1, provides a basis for using the descriptor Meets Standard on his electronic report card. This would be an example of using performance assessment for summative purposes.

1.3 The Curriculum Planning-Teaching-Assessing Cycle

Focus Question What are simple definitions of assessment, measurement, tests, and evaluation?

To make the best possible use of all forms of educational assessment, three fundamentally important points need to be kept in mind.

  1. Assessment is an integral part of the teaching–learning process.
  2. Assessment is not simply something that teachers do to students; it is something that is accomplished collaboratively by learners and teachers.
  3. The most important purpose of educational assessment is to enhance student motivation and learning and improve instructional approaches.

Figure 1.2 illustrates these three points.

Note that although assessing, planning, and teaching are shown as distinct activities, they constantly overlap.

Assessment occurs before, during, and after teaching. Planning and modifying approaches to instruction and to learning—all of which rely on good assessment—are continuous, ongoing processes.

The most useful assessments for instructional purposes are informal and frequent ungraded assessments that inform both the teacher's and the students' minute-to-minute and day-to-day activities.

Some Definitions

Many of the commonly used terms in assessment literature—terms like test, assessment, measurement, and evaluation—are treated as though they are synonymous. They aren't. Table 1.2 briefly summarizes the differences.

Table 1.2
Some definitions
Term Definition
Test A specific instrument or procedure that measures student progress and achievement and that may or may not produce quantifiable results.
Measurement A procedure or instrument that produces quantifiable results.
Assessment A general term that includes testing, measuring, and evaluating activities.
Evaluation An appraisal based on specified criteria.
Grade Usually a number or a letter that summarizes a student's achievement.

Test

In education, the word test typically refers to specific instruments or procedures designed to measure student achievement or progress, or to identify various student characteristics.

Tests typically consist of a sample of behaviors (responses) that serve as a basis for making inferences. For example, a ninth-grade algebra final test does not examine students' complete body of algebraic knowledge or their ability to solve all problems related to the entire course. Instead, the test consists of a representative sample of problems. If a student responds correctly to 84% of the problems, the examiner infers that the student also would have responded correctly to 84% of the entire pool of relevant problems.

Test results are often quantitative in that they provide a numerical measure, such as 90%, or a grade with clear meaning, such as a B. However, sometimes they are qualitative, in that they describe some particular quality such as Meeting all standards or Excellent descriptive paragraph. Whether tests are developed by the teacher or are commercially prepared, they are a relatively formal approach to assessment. They are commonly used in placement and summative assessment but less so in formative assessment, which tends to be more informal.

Measurement

Measurements are usually quantitative: They are used to indicate an amount. Thus, I measure the length of my foot in inches or centimeters; but I can test its suitability for running by putting it through a series of movements.

Measurement in education is usually based on the results of tests. Strictly speaking, educational measurement is a set of procedures for manipulating and interpreting test results.

For example, educational measurement might involve making decisions about specific levels of performance that are acceptable in a unit test or that identify learners who need assistance.

Assessment

Assessment is a more general term.

In an educational setting, assessment refers to all the activities—including testing and measuring—undertaken by a teacher to obtain information about students' characteristics, their progress and achievement, and the effectiveness of instructional methods in relation to desired outcomes.

There is a strong belief that in the 21st century, assessment should be a collaborative process that engages both learner and teacher, and perhaps involves parents as well. In this sense, assessment reflects a commitment to achievement and progress, and recognizes that assessment-based feedback is an essential component of the teaching–learning cycle.

Educational assessment is no longer viewed as a mysterious, secretive, teacher-controlled process. Today's learner should be able to access assessment-based, formative feedback in collaboration with the teacher—perhaps by means of face-to-face interactions or via online, digital technology.

Evaluation

Literally, evaluation refers to the process of making a value judgment. It may involve deciding that something is good or bad, moral or immoral, effective or ineffective.

In education, however, evaluation is seldom as subjective as this definition might imply.

Evaluation is usually based on the results of one or more assessments, and it often takes the form of an appraisal based on whether certain criteria have been met.

For example, determining whether a student qualifies for special needs services in primary grades usually requires the use of specific evaluation criteria related to speech and language difficulties, social and emotional problems, or behavioral problems.

Grade

Grades are generally numbers, letters, or verbal descriptors that describe a particular rank or quality.

Thus, there are different grades of beef, lumber, wheat, paper—and even students.

In education, grades are summative rather than formative.

They are usually based on the results of tests or on other forms of assessment. Occasionally, as we will see in Chapter 11, they might also reflect other factors such as performance on assignments or class participation.

What We Measure

Assessments, measurements, and evaluations are a huge part of everyday life. You have been subjected to assessments of one kind or another for your whole life.

Everyday Assessment

In all likelihood, within a few minutes of being born, a nurse or attending physician gave you the well-known Apgar test—a test that assessed your general appearance (blue or pink); your pulse (heart rate); your grimace (reflexive reactions such as coughing, sneezing, crying, or grimacing); your activity level (muscle tone described as limp, weak, or strong); and your respiration.

The maximum score on this test is 10: a score of 9 or 10 is considered excellent, 7–8 is good, 4–6 is intermediate, and lower scores are poor. Incidentally, over 90% of U.S. infants score 9 or 10, and fewer than 0.5% score below 4 (Fei et al., 2013). Had your score been zero on this first test, you would in all likelihood not be reading about it today (see Table 1.3).

Table 1.3

The Apgar test is one of many assessments you likely had before you even started school. For example, perhaps your vision was tested, the condition of your teeth appraised, or your health evaluated. Your general developmental progression might have been formally assessed by one or more developmental experts. It would most certainly have been informally evaluated by proud or concerned parents: I wonder why Luca isn't walking yet; Tansy isn't even 8 months and she can walk!

Educational Assessment

In many ways, life is a series of formal and informal assessments.

Some show how well you are performing compared to your peers; others appraise your performance relative to some standard or norm that describes expected or average behavior or achievement.

And of course, the preschool and school experiences that follow bring with them a whole range of new assessments. At the very least, schools typically measure student achievement; most also assess various kinds of aptitude or ability. They might also examine personality, vocational and career interests, and performance in different subject areas.

Some of the assessments used involve formal measuring instruments, often produced by professional test developers. Others are constructed by teachers.

Often, too, assessment is based on more informal kinds of observations made both in and outside the classroom. Some of the various types of school assessments are described in Table 1.4.

Table 1.4
Different kinds of educational assessment
Kind of assessment What is evaluated Examples
Intelligence or ability General intellectual potential Formal, standardized intelligence tests such as the Stanford–Binet Intelligence Test or the Cognitive Abilities Test
Aptitude More specific abilities (aptitudes) Formal tests that look at specific skills in areas such as reading, abstract reasoning, or numerical skills. Examples include the Common Aptitudes Test and the Xavier Aptitude Test.
Personality Traits and characteristics that make each individual unique Formal personality tests such as the Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory (MMPI) or the Five-Factor Personality Inventory
Performance The learner's performances or products in a variety of settings, many of which are as close to the constraints and demands of real life as possible Assessments of performances or products such as when a person writes an essay, conducts an experiment, solves a math problem, sings, exhibits a painting, plays a musical instrument, or recites a poem
Achievement The learner's accomplishments and general proficiency in various areas Teacher-made tests, as well as more formal county- or statewide achievement tests; may be professionally developed, general achievement tests such as the General Educational Development (GED) or the California Achievement Test

How We Use Results

The chief purpose of any educational assessment is to foster learning. However, each of the broad assessment approaches described in Table 1.4 might serve a variety of purposes in the classroom, including the following.

  • determining whether learning goals are being met
  • assessing the effectiveness of instructional methods
  • gauging the extent to which learners are engaged in learning
  • monitoring student achievement
  • providing information for parents
  • satisfying administrative and judicial requirements
  • appraising learner satisfaction and happiness
  • determining student background information and readiness to learn
  • helping learners make wise decisions
  • developing self-assessment skills among learners
  • making decisions about student advancement and placement
  • advising students regarding career and vocational decisions
  • tailoring instructional methods to student aptitudes and interests

What Information Learners Need

Most of the uses for educational assessment are not mutually exclusive. A single, well-constructed, teacher-made, end-of-unit test might serve every one of the purposes just listed.

However, the kind of assessment information the learner needs is quite different from the assessment information that might be given to parents.

Interested parties such as parents, prospective employers, and university admissions officers are mainly interested in summative information that allows them to compare and evaluate. That is, they want to know what the learner has achieved relative to educational goals and relative to other learners. On that basis, they can predict what the learner is capable of, provide advice about educational and career options, and make decisions about placement.

In contrast, what learners need in the day-to-day business of learning is immediate, personalized, confidential information about their learning. They need formative information that will enhance their ongoing development.
